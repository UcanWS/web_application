Using -ing Form and Infinitive with "to"

-ing Form vs. Infinitive with "to"

What are they?

  • -ing form: Verb + -ing (e.g. reading, dancing, going)
  • Infinitive with to: to + base verb (e.g. to eat, to study, to be)

1. Verbs followed by -ing

Some verbs are followed by the -ing form.

Common verbs: enjoy, avoid, finish, mind, suggest, keep, imagine

  • I enjoy reading books.
  • She avoids eating junk food.
  • He suggested going for a walk.

2. Verbs followed by "to" + infinitive

Some verbs are followed by the infinitive form with to.

Common verbs: want, decide, need, hope, agree, plan, learn, offer

  • They want to travel this summer.
  • She decided to quit her job.
  • I hope to see you soon.

Time Expressions with Present Perfect and Past Simple

Present Perfect is used to talk about actions that happened at an unspecified time before now, or actions that started in the past and continue in the present. Time expressions commonly used with Present Perfect include:

  • since (starting point in time): She has lived here since 2010.
  • for (duration of time): I have known him for 5 years.
  • ever (at any time): Have you ever been to Paris?
  • never (not at any time): I have never seen that movie.
  • just (recently): They have just finished their meal.
  • already (before now): She has already left.
  • yet (negative or question form): We haven‚Äôt seen that movie yet.

Past Simple is used to talk about actions that happened at a specific time in the past. Time expressions commonly used with Past Simple include:

  • yesterday: I saw him yesterday.
  • last week/month/year: We visited them last year.
  • in (year/month/season): They went on vacation in July.
  • ago: I met him five years ago.
  • when: We were in Paris when I was a child.

Key differences:

  • Present Perfect is used for actions with relevance to the present moment. Example: I have finished my homework.
  • Past Simple is used for actions that happened at a specific time in the past. Example: I finished my homework yesterday.
